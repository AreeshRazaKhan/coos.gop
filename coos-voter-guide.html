A letter from the committee

Three races. Three Republicans. One ballot that matters.

This guide exists because Coos County voters deserve more than yard signs and slogans. Local elections shape the schools your kids attend, the roads you drive, the deputies who answer your call, and whether the family-owned mill, fishing operation, contractor, or rancher down the road can keep working without permission slips from Salem.

In the spring of 2026, three Republican candidates put their names forward to lead Coos County and represent House District 9 in the State Legislature. Each one was sent the same questionnaire — twenty-five questions on public safety, taxes, education, the economy, election integrity, and the issues neighbors actually ask about most.

The candidates featured in this guide chose to answer in full and on the record. Their opponents in each race — also Republicans on the May ballot — were given the same opportunity and chose not to participate. Where they did not respond, we have noted it plainly. We have not paraphrased a word of what was submitted; the answers in this booklet are the candidates' own.

Read it cover to cover or jump to the race that matters to you. Compare answers side by side. Make up your own mind. Then bring this with you when you fill out your ballot, and pass it to a neighbor when you are done.

Coos County is worth fighting for. The first fight is showing up to vote.

Faithfully, Friends of Coos County Republicans PAC #24520 Political Action Committee

State House District 9

State Representative · Claire Lynn

Republican Primary1 of 2 responded
Responded in full

Claire Lynn

Republican Candidate · State Representative HD9

A former three-sport varsity athlete with a year of college softball behind her, Claire Lynn brings a track record of work inside the Oregon House Republican Office and on the front lines of grassroots campaigns across the state. She is running to be a steady, principled conservative voice for Coos County in Salem.

clairefororegon.com
Claire Lynn — in her own words
1.Oregon ranks #1 in sex offenders per capita. What measures would you propose to address this issue, including tougher sentencing, registration requirements, or community safety initiatives?+
My observation is that public safety is just not made a priority in the State Legislature. The fact is our state spends double what we spend on our Oregon State Police for healthcare for non-citizens. That needs to change. Budgets reflect priorities — and public safety must absolutely be made a priority in our state budget. We also know that there are thousands of sex offenders living within our communities that need to be reevaluated to determine their likelihood to reoffend. Republicans in the Legislature have had proposals to fund staffing to clear the sex offender risk assessment backlog, expand the definition of "victim" so more survivors could access critical information, and ensure that convicted sex offenders moving to Oregon would be classified under the same risk level they had in other states. Unfortunately, these common sense proposals were blocked by the Democrat Supermajority. Oregon must get back to a place where we value victims of crime more than the perpetrators.
2.Do you support full cooperation with federal immigration agencies, such as ICE?+
Yes — cooperate with federal authorities.
3.Do you believe illegal immigrants should be eligible for programs funded by taxpayers?+
No — they should not have access to these programs.
4.Do you believe men can become women, or vice versa?+
No.
5.What is your position on allowing biological males to participate in women's sports or use women's locker rooms?+
As a former three-sport high school varsity athlete and someone who played a year of college softball, I am a firm believer that we have a responsibility to protect women's sports and women's spaces from biological men. You can count on me to be an unwavering advocate on this issue in the State Legislature, just as Representative Wright has been.
6.Should taxpayer funds be used for gender-affirming procedures, including for minors?+
Absolutely not — but unfortunately, the current reality in this state is that we are covering every element of it. In the 2023 Legislative Session, I was intimately involved in fighting House Bill 2002 which expanded the list of procedures covered by taxpayers to include facial feminization surgery and laser hair removal.
7.How would you work to bring businesses back to Oregon, particularly in Coos County?+
I will evaluate every piece of legislation that comes before me in Salem through a simple lens: Will this place new burdens on private businesses? Will this require Oregonians to pay more? These are straightforward questions — but they matter. As the seventh most regulated state in the nation, our Legislature should be focused on identifying opportunities to reduce unnecessary red tape, not adding to it. By cutting outdated or excessive regulations, we can help make life more affordable and create an environment where businesses — and the communities they support — can thrive.
8.Are Urban Renewal Agencies helping or hurting local communities?+
Neutral. I think the effectiveness of Urban Renewal Agencies depends on the plan and personnel within each local city. I believe there are Urban Renewal Agencies that do very good work to attract people and visitors to a community, but I also think there are scenarios when this looks like the government picking winners and losers in private industry rather than allowing the free market to work.
9.What is your view on various taxes and fees, such as disposal fees, bag fees, gas taxes, corporate activity taxes, and estate (death) taxes? Would you support reductions or repeals?+
When Oregonians say they cannot afford new taxes, like we recently saw with Governor Kotek's $4.3 billion gas tax, I believe we have a responsibility to listen. I was proud to push back against this proposal through my work in the Oregon House Republican Office, and then again as a volunteer collecting signatures for the referendum. House Republicans have had common sense policy proposals to raise the threshold on the Corporate Activity Tax from $1 million to $5 million. We have also tried to raise the Oregon Estate Tax exemption from $1 million to $5 million. Unfortunately, these proposals are frequently denied by Democrats in the Legislature. I will absolutely advocate for any kind of repeal or reduction whenever possible.
10.What is your position on the PCIP (Pacific Coast Intermodal Port) project? Do you support its development, and how would it be a net benefit to Coos County?+
I am supportive of anything that's going to bring a single job to Coos County or House District 9. I also believe that anytime you are spending millions in both state and federal taxpayer dollars, it's important to have an elected official who is willing to ask tough questions about how those dollars are being spent. I believe I am the best positioned candidate in this race to do so.
11.Do you support requiring photo ID for casting a ballot in elections?+
Yes — require photo ID.
12.What are you doing to help more conservatives get elected in Oregon?+
I have spent much of my professional life working to get more conservatives elected in Oregon, including as a volunteer on numerous campaigns. My husband and I have put up more field signs for candidates than we can count, and I have knocked on hundreds of doors for campaigns prior to now running my own.
13.Regarding HB 3115 (which allows time and place restrictions on camping), do you believe it has been effective? Should cities be required to provide designated areas for the homeless, and what other solutions would you propose?+
House Bill 3115, which essentially legalized tent cities in Oregon, has been devastating. House Republicans have introduced legislation in past sessions to repeal this law. I am hopeful because Oregon Business and Industry has filed what they are calling the "Local Control & Safety Act" to repeal House Bill 3115 via a ballot measure.
14.Are you in favor of reopening or increasing funding for mental health institutions to address issues like addiction and mental illness?+
Yes — open more institutions.
15.Should graduation standards in Oregon schools be lowered or raised? Why?+
We absolutely must reinstate graduation standards in Oregon schools. We are doing a complete and utter disservice to our kids.
16.How would you help reverse the dropping enrollment rates in Oregon schools?+
Oregon ranks 47th in the nation for our quality of K-12 education. I am supportive of common sense legislation that would require students be held back if they can't demonstrate grade-level proficiency in core subjects by third grade.
17.Do you support school choice policies, such as allowing education funding to follow the child to private or charter schools?+
Yes — support.
18.Should there be more limits and regulations on hunting and fishing in Oregon?+
No — less.
19.Should there be more limits and regulations on firearms?+
No — less.
20.How would you balance the county/state budget while addressing key priorities like public safety which is severely underfunded in Oregon?+
As I said above, budgets reflect priorities. Public safety must come first.
21.Should the Oregon Department of Transportation (ODOT) receive more or less funding? How would you prioritize transportation projects in Coos County?+
Our state government needs to learn to live within its means like Oregon families have to. ODOT spends $29 million on a Climate and Equity Division. There is not a shortage of funds — they are being mismanaged.
22.What is one thing you feel you can reasonably accomplish in your first year in office?+
One thing I feel I can reasonably accomplish in my first year is prioritizing transparent communication with my district and helping people stay engaged in the process.
23.Do you support term limits for elected officials? If so, what limits would you propose?+
While I'm running for the state legislature, I absolutely believe we need limits federally.
24.Who is your political role model, and why?+
My political role model is President Ronald Reagan for his fierce love of America and his belief that — despite our flaws — we really are still that "shining city on a hill."
25.What is your favorite thing about Coos County?+
My favorite thing about Coos County are all of the down-to-earth and hardworking people, as well as the profound sense of entrepreneurship.

Brandi Martindale

Republican Candidate · Commissioner Position 2

A grassroots organizer who has spent the last two years opening politics to the public — hosting speaking opportunities, meet-and-greets, and coaching first-time candidates. Brandi Martindale is running to make Coos County a more accountable, more transparent, and decidedly less hospitable place for sexual predators and bureaucratic drift.

brandiforcoos.com
Brandi Martindale — in her own words
1.Oregon ranks #1 in sex offenders per capita. What measures would you propose to address this issue?+
A county commissioner has limited power regarding sentencing, but there are changes Coos can make that will improve community safety. I will do what I can to make Coos County a sex-offender unfriendly place, both on paper and culturally.
2.Do you support full cooperation with federal immigration agencies, such as ICE?+
Yes — cooperate with federal authorities.
3.Do you believe illegal immigrants should be eligible for programs funded by taxpayers?+
No — they should not have access to these programs.
4.Do you believe men can become women, or vice versa?+
No.
5.What is your position on allowing biological males to participate in women's sports or use women's locker rooms?+
Allowing males into female intimate spaces is a violation of our most basic biological boundary. Women must have the right to same-sex sport, or females lose their sport completely — I cite shewon.org.
6.Should taxpayer funds be used for gender-affirming procedures, including for minors?+
No.
7.How would you work to bring businesses back to Oregon, particularly in Coos County?+
Bringing business back to Coos is going to require networking with current industries to identify opportunity. I am highly skilled at crafting solutions that are win-wins-wins.
8.Are Urban Renewal Agencies helping or hurting local communities?+
Neutral. URAs can be good or bad, depending on how they are applied — like most things.
9.What is your view on various taxes and fees? Would you support reductions or repeals?+
I will always support reductions and repeals of taxes and fees when feasible. I believe it's an ethical imperative of elected officials to actively seek ways to do so.
10.What is your position on the PCIP (Pacific Coast Intermodal Port) project?+
The PCIP has the opportunity to bring export and import economy to Coos County. I have no intention of blocking the project or preventing it from moving forward in any way.
11.Do you support requiring photo ID for casting a ballot in elections?+
Yes — require photo ID.
12.What are you doing to help more conservatives get elected in Oregon?+
To help get conservatives elected, I am working to open politics to the public by hosting speaking opportunities, meet and greets, creating public content to engage voters, and offering coaching where I can to help candidates succeed.
13.Regarding HB 3115 ("Right to Camp"), do you believe it has been effective?+
I do believe HB 3115 was a horrifically inhumane solution to a problem directly caused by Oregon legislators who voted to close Shutter Creek. I want to see full reinstitutionalization.
14.Are you in favor of reopening or increasing funding for mental health institutions?+
Yes — open more institutions.
15.Should graduation standards in Oregon schools be lowered or raised? Why?+
Graduation standards should be raised to meet national standards. Standards must rise but that rise needs to take place within the curriculum itself.
16.How would you help reverse the dropping enrollment rates in Oregon schools?+
Stop teaching sexuality to children in school and people will send their kids to public school again.
17.Do you support school choice policies?+
Yes — support.
18.Should there be more limits and regulations on hunting and fishing in Oregon?+
No — less.
19.Should there be more limits and regulations on firearms?+
Remain the same.
20.How would you balance the county/state budget while addressing key priorities?+
As commissioner, my financial power is pretty limited to the general fund. My focus will be looking for every small opportunity to do things more effectively and efficiently.
21.Should ODOT receive more or less funding?+
I don't think the answer to DOT funding is a more or less one — I think it's the how and why. As Commissioner, there is not a lot of control I have over state spending, but I can be an outspoken voice for greater transparency, accountability, and disciplined prioritization.
22.What is one thing you feel you can reasonably accomplish in your first year in office?+
One thing I think I can accomplish in my first year in office as commissioner is creating a cultural shift around sexual predators in our county.
23.Do you support term limits for elected officials? If so, what limits would you propose?+
I fully support term limits and I believe two terms should be the standard limit for all elected positions.
24.Who is your political role model, and why?+
My political role model is John N. Kennedy. As a person, he is honest, straightforward, and courageous.
25.What is your favorite thing about Coos County?+
My favorite thing about Coos County is our proximity to nature. We are surrounded by mountains, forests, ocean, and everything in between.

Richard "Rick" Coleman

Republican Candidate · Commissioner Position 3

A lifelong Coos County resident whose memories of Sunny Hill School and the working communities that built this place inform every position he takes. Rick Coleman is running on a plain-spoken platform: tougher consequences for predators, lower taxes for working families, real schools that teach reading, writing, and arithmetic, and a county jail that actually opens its doors.

facebook.com/rickcoleman
Richard Coleman — in his own words
1.Oregon ranks #1 in sex offenders per capita. What measures would you propose to address this issue?+
I would definitely have tougher laws and sentencing on these people. Registration requirements for sure, and never allow pedophiles to work where children may be.
2.Do you support full cooperation with federal immigration agencies, such as ICE?+
Yes — cooperate with federal authorities.
3.Do you believe illegal immigrants should be eligible for programs funded by taxpayers?+
No — they should not have access to these programs.
4.Do you believe men can become women, or vice versa?+
No.
5.What is your position on allowing biological males to participate in women's sports or use women's locker rooms?+
Biological males should not be in women's sports or use their locker rooms.
6.Should taxpayer funds be used for gender-affirming procedures, including for minors?+
Gender-affirming procedures are not life-or-death procedures. This is an elective procedure and should be paid for by the individual. This should never be allowed for minors, since their brains are not fully developed until they're in their mid-twenties.
7.How would you work to bring businesses back to Oregon, particularly in Coos County?+
I would try to make our area more attractive to businesses. At the state level, we need to become more business friendly. Quit taxing them to death.
8.Are Urban Renewal Agencies helping or hurting local communities?+
Hurting.
9.What is your view on various taxes and fees? Would you support reductions or repeals?+
I would definitely support reductions and repeals of these taxes. These were sneaky ways for the state to get a sales tax.
10.What is your position on the PCIP (Pacific Coast Intermodal Port) project?+
We need some sort of living-wage industry in Coos County. I'm just not convinced this will happen.
11.Do you support requiring photo ID for casting a ballot in elections?+
Yes — require photo ID.
12.What are you doing to help more conservatives get elected in Oregon?+
Talking to people and letting them know voting is important.
13.Regarding HB 3115 ("Right to Camp"), do you believe it has been effective?+
No, not effective at all. I believe this brings more crime into communities. I believe mental health and addiction issues are the biggest part of the homeless problem. We need to reopen facilities to help these people.
14.Are you in favor of reopening or increasing funding for mental health institutions?+
Yes — open more institutions.
15.Should graduation standards in Oregon schools be lowered or raised? Why?+
Raise the standards. It's a complete travesty to allow our youth to graduate not knowing how to read and write.
16.How would you help reverse the dropping enrollment rates in Oregon schools?+
Get rid of DEI. Get back to real teaching. Reading, writing, and arithmetic.
17.Do you support school choice policies?+
Yes — support.
18.Should there be more limits and regulations on hunting and fishing in Oregon?+
No — less.
19.Should there be more limits and regulations on firearms?+
No — less.
20.How would you balance the county/state budget while addressing key priorities?+
Public safety should be funded first and cut last.
21.Should ODOT receive more or less funding?+
I think ODOT needs to trim the fat. No raise in the budget. I think road maintenance is a good place to start here in Coos County. Pulling debris out of ditches would definitely help keep the roads from getting undermined.
22.What is one thing you feel you can reasonably accomplish in your first year in office?+
Getting the conversation started and the ball rolling on getting our jail opened up.
23.Do you support term limits for elected officials? If so, what limits would you propose?+
Yes, no more than two terms in elected positions.
24.Who is your political role model, and why?+
I'm not sure that I truly have one, but if I had to pick, George Washington comes to mind. His refusal to do more than two terms is a great standard to go by.
25.What is your favorite thing about Coos County?+
This is where I grew up. It just feels like home to me. My memories of going to Sunny Hill School.
